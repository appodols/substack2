When I told a neighbor girl about it, she showed me how to play street hockey. Once, in a heart-stopping moment, a semi-truck zoomed toward us. We cleared the road. I realized my toy was still in the street. I braced for it to be destroyed and when the truck passed, I was relieved to see the toy was unscathed. Some weeks later, the puck shattered when the other kids and I were playing with it. That was the end of the toy.
If youve ever met a kid with no toys, they will cherish even the ones they dont understand.
Ive never held sentimental attachment to any objects since then.
Toys were scarce, so I was grateful for the one I had.
Later, when free time became scarce, I became grateful for that, too.
Ten years after discovering that hockey puck under my bed, I had completed basic training at Lackland Air Force Base, Texas. After every aspect of existence was tightly monitored and regulated, suddenly freedom became a luxury to be cherished.
I distinctly recall my roommate playing Guitar Hero on Xbox 360 and saying, How weird is it that were playing Xbox when two days ago we had to get permission to just read a letter from home?
This, by the way, is one reason Im dubious of people who claim free will doesnt exist. Maybe it doesnt exist in some abstract metaphysical sense. But in concrete reality, if youve experienced the feeling of having your actions coerced by another person  under the threat of swift imprisonment or death, youll know the difference between what it feels like to have free will versus not. If free will doesnt exist, then freedom no longer means anything. Which then paves the way toward authoritarianism.
A while back I read Robert Samuelsons book,The Good Life and its Discontents: The American Dream in the Age of Entitlement.One point he argued was that Americans in the postwar era had come to believe in a teleological vision of life. People began taking for granted a constant upward trajectory of both material and psychological improvement.
A while back I read Robert Samuelsons book,
One point he argued was that Americans in the postwar era had come to believe in a teleological vision of life. People began taking for granted a constant upward trajectory of both material and psychological improvement.
The pursuit of ever-increasing affluence and convenience eventually produces diminishing returns. People who expect relentless forward movement toward perfection will inevitably be disappointed. Our disappointment has led to fault-finding and finger-pointing as we attempt to pinpoint who or what is to blame for depriving us of our birthright.
All we can think about is what we feel was owed to us and wrongly withheld. Regardless of how much we already have.
Scarcity breeds gratitude. In an environment of scarcity, occasional windfalls increase happiness because people are well aware that things could be worse. But a lifetime of prosperity and assumed abundance often coincides with ingratitude. Unless we regularly remind ourselves to run a plausible counterfactual in which we are suddenly dispossessed and made to live in meager circumstances. Without gratitude, happiness is impossible.
I wasnt guaranteed any of this. It could so easily have been different. But its here for now, and thats enough. Im thankful for all of it.
