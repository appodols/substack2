In the past 10 years theres been a linguistic shift where instead of saying Someone told me XYZ, people now say Had someone tell me XYZ. An extension of main character syndrome where people place themselves at the center of everything and think in terms of consumption. Had.
One has interactions in the same way one has experiences or possessions. People increasingly view others comments as objects to be consumed and then later exploited for social media content.
We live in a quasi-surveillance state with no private moments. The weird thing is how this is bottom up rather than top down. People voluntarily do this, with no state coercion. Recall that in East Germany and other communist states, there existed people who actually relished the fact that they were deputized to spy on their neighbors and gleefully ratted them out to the authorities.
InThe Culture of Narcissism, Christopher Lasch wrote that, Modern life is so mediated by electronic images that we cannot help responding to others as if their actions- and our own- were being transmitted to an unseen audience or stored for scrutiny at some later time.

Lasch pointed out that modern life is saturated with electronic images and performances, so we cant help but act as if our every move is being broadcast. Were both guard and inmate in our own digital panopticon.
With social media, every innocuous personal interaction is fodder for the rudest people on earth to mock in a public forum. The everydaywelcoming a guest, cooking dinner, or noticing a piece of dcorno longer belongs to the moment. Instead, its framed as content for likes, shares, or retweets.
Most people arent like this. Remember thatonly10% of people produce 80% of the content you see online. Within this 10% are people who have extreme personality types who are happy to forego genuine human connection in exchange for quick bursts of digital approval. Instead of responding with warmth, such people are happy to seize the moment to share a snarky post, promoting their online persona at the expense of basic courtesy. Scrolling through replies to a post like this, youll often see comments along the lines of Straight men are so clueless, or Coworkers are the worst, which is just another way to indulge in your darkest impulses by finding a socially acceptable target for your derision. The stronger the put-down, the bigger the digital reward.
Angry people are constantly looking for targets who they can be praised for mocking. If you say Women are so annoying you will be called a misogynist. But if you say White women are so annoying you will be applauded. Which is why you will now see both the left and the right post critical comments specifically about white women.
Anyway, if I like your stuff can yield that kind of dismissive treatment, no wonder more and more people are hesitant to be genuine. Who wants to risk being the target of a viral joke?
I suspect this is one reason among many why young people arent pairing up anymore. If you express interest on the apps or by text or DM, the other person can screenshot whatever you send and publicly mock you. If you ask someone out in person, you also risk being publicly scorned.
Of course, the odds of this happening are low. But all people see are the viral tweets and think the risks are much higher than they really are. A tweet about a single event gets a few thousand retweets and people adjust their behavior to lower the risk of being publicly humiliated.

Privacy is the enemy of profit for the system. Genuine human connection doesnt fuel what some have termed limbic capitalism, or the idea that the digital economy thrives on keeping people in a state of heightened emotional stimulationangry, anxious, addicted, and engaged. Privacy, by contrast, is a quiet withdrawal from the marketplace. A private person, one who doesnt document their every move or convert their relationships into content, disrupts the system by refusing to participate.
Every aspect of life is dragged into the public domain. Social media platforms, reality TV, dating apps, OnlyFans, personal brandingeverything is designed to make people more visible, more legible, and ultimately, more monetizable. A person who insists on keeping their thoughts, their relationships, or their emotions private is, in this framework, almost subversive. Their refusal to be consumed makes them unprofitable.
Mocking people for a specific behavior online reduces the likelihood of others engaging in that same behavior. I suppose Im sometimes guilty of this myself.
In my defense, though, the world would be a better place if fewer Harvard grads bragged about their alma mater. The world would be a worse place if people stopped complimenting others homes and personal possessions.
