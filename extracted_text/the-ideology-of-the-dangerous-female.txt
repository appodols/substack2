(2024) takes place in the nineteenth century. As a viewer, youre plunged into a world where the ideals of the Western Enlightenment (science, rationality, a clockwork universe that obeys predictable materialist laws) are powerless against the esoteric and the diabolical. In this reimagining, director Robert Eggersarguably one of the best filmmakers working todayadapts the 1922 silent classic for a contemporary audience. It showcases an uneasy blend of dread, sexual undertones, and primal myth.
The inciting incident that kicks the story into motion involves Ellen Hutter, the films central female protagonist and wife to Thomas Hutter, the male protagonist.
Far from being an innocent bystander, Ellen is portrayed as having been entangled with supernatural evil since maidenhood as a young woman. In the opening scene, you see Ellen summoning Count Orlok (Nosferatu) in a ritualistic act of curiosity that beckons darkness. The resulting horror arises as much from her desire as from Count Orloks intrusion.
At a surface level, it is the supernatural Count Orlok, with his long fingers and interest in bloodsucking, who unsettles us.
At a more latent and unconscious level, though, the film underscores one of the oldest irrational fears housed in the male psyche: womans supposed alliance with chaos and danger.

For instance, Gilmore reports that in classical Hindu lore, it is not so much the imaginary sorceress of folklore and myth as the normal, everyday woman who is the cause of sin in the world. Ordinary women (inadvertently or unconsciously) lead men astray, disrupt order, and cultivate conflict. In Hinduism, human women are used as the specific instrument of the gods to corrupt sages and wise men and to turn men into demons. Thus deceived by women, men lose their minds and are then preyed upon by the evil forces of the supernatural world. Such beliefs were also held in ancient Greece, medieval Europe, and India, as well as among remote Amazonian tribes, that woman is the genius of darkness, foul as falsehood itself.
This objectified image of mystical, unpredictable, female danger, allied with the horrors of the supernatural, is nearly universal in the worlds religions (think of Eve accepting the forbidden fruit from the serpent in the otherwise idyllic Garden of Eden), as well as in secular folklore.
Gilmore points out that many faiths issue dire warnings to boys and grown men of the transforming powers of this menacing female.
Many cultures have mythical folklore of a siren or female sea serpent, which represents an archetypal dream image: that of losing control, being powerless, and falling prey to powers beyond rational control. The male sailor or voyager, bravely plying the surface of nature in search of knowledge or wisdom, is suddenly and without warning sucked into oblivion by terrifying supernatural creatures that rise up from the murky depths. The siren or mermaid is ambiguous: at once sexually seductive and monstrously evil.
This ambivalent nightmare, which many men share regardless of social status or culture, metaphorically links male fears of erotic indulgence with subsequent punishment and death. The anthropologist David Gilmore writes that these female sprites entertain devils and seduce and defile men.
Across cultures, folklore frequently regards femininity as the gateway through which demonic forces prey on hapless men.
The siren archetype, the mermaid, and the succubus tap into a core nightmare in the male psyche: losing control, succumbing to the unknown, and facing ones own destruction, all while under the spell of the most powerful force on earthfemale sexual allure.
Ellen is responsible for calling forth demonic forces, which threatens the stability of her society (symbolic of masculine order). She is also (partiallymore on this later) responsible for the demise of Count Orlok (another male).
Ellens journey sets the story in motion by inviting Count Orlok into the human realm. Shes also the one who must deal with the consequences of her hubris. Weirdly, though, her arc is undercut by a muddied conclusion. On paper, its supposed to be about seizing agency: Ellen chooses to sacrifice herself to distract Orlok, presumably saving her town and her husband.
But the film explicitly tells us that Orloks defeat hinges on him needing his coffin for rest; the men (led by a particularly manipulative Willem Dafoe character) manage to destroy that coffin and burn his refuge to cinders, sealing his fate. Ellens ultimate sacrificewhich includes sharing one final sexual encounter with the vampire, who has tormented her and her husbandcomes across as unnecessary and absurd.
Theres even a moment when Ellen, as both temptress and truth-teller, tells her husband Thomas that Nosferatu was a superior lover.
Nosferatu(2024) is a horror story revolving around two timeless male anxieties: cuckoldry and sexual violation at the hands of a deviant force. Watching Thomas Hutter be drained of his life force by Orlok, only to have his wife openly pine for that same monster, is bleak and nightmarish.
(2024) is a horror story revolving around two timeless male anxieties: cuckoldry and sexual violation at the hands of a deviant force. Watching Thomas Hutter be drained of his life force by Orlok, only to have his wife openly pine for that same monster, is bleak and nightmarish.
You cant help but laugh at poor Thomas: battered, drained of his blood, and saddled with the knowledge that his wife had been pining for a demon she herself unleashed. Ellens arc, in contrast, is supposed to be about transgression and empowerment.
Willem Dafoe is superb as always in his role as the professor who seeks to understand and combat Count Orlok.
Despite its flaws, the film does succeed in depicting a rich portrait of a budding modern civilization tragically unprepared for the forces that lurk beyond the Enlightenment. Beyond science and logic.
