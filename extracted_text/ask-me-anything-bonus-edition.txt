Interestingly,Troubleddid not make the NYT list, despite outselling some of the new titles on that list this week. Even my publisher thinks something is fishy, and they are inquiring about this oversight. I read about how these lists are organized. The USA Today list and the Publishers Weekly list seem to be based primarily (though not entirely) on raw sales. In contrast, the NYT list cares about where the books are being purchased (e.g., fancy independent bookstores in NY and SF). Of course, they care about sales (to a point), but the tastemakers who curate the list also concentrate on image and fit. Similar to holistic admissions. To draw a rough analogy: the USA Today bestseller list is akin to MIT or Caltech; the NYT list is more like Harvard or Yale. Ill write more about this later.

Despite the hectic past several days, some of which I spent battling illness, Im feeling energized and pleased with how things have gone with the book. So far in total Ive done 48 media appearances across podcast, radio, television, and print. More to go. During book launch week, I think I had what Amy describes here:
I was ready to power through the illness for book promo. Then I literally lost my voice and had to cancel meetings for an entire day. On subsequent days Id record 3 or 4 podcasts in a day and then spend the rest of evening mainlining Robitussin and shivering under a blanket. I recall reading a quote in a Ryan Holiday book If you do everything, youll win. I was glad to be young and healthy enough todespite my temporary infirmitydo everything I could to promote this book. James Clear says a book launch should feel like an event. To have a successful launch, Clear describes what he calls a concentrated strike, or the idea that for two weeks or so it should feel like an author is everywhere. You open social media, or your favorite podcast, or speak to your friend, and it seems like everyone is speaking about the book, which makes you want to learn about it. Then you open Amazon and see that lots of people have reviewed it, which gets you to finally order it. There is an element of showmanship and theater here which I learned not from academics, but from internet writers. As a new author (or new anything in any creative domain with lots of competition), you need to have high agency and hustle. A question asked on the Google form.  Jan asks: Thank you, Rob, for your excellent work, and I look forward to reading your new book that I ultimately ordered and to be sent to Kazakhstan. My question to you is the following: To what extent is it worthwhile to pursue PhD degree if one aspires to be a good writer and consistently write on a particular subject for the public audience, as you do? How does one create conditions that make the pursuit of PhD a significant booster for good and consistent writing?
A question asked on the Google form.  Jan asks: Thank you, Rob, for your excellent work, and I look forward to reading your new book that I ultimately ordered and to be sent to Kazakhstan. My question to you is the following: To what extent is it worthwhile to pursue PhD degree if one aspires to be a good writer and consistently write on a particular subject for the public audience, as you do? How does one create conditions that make the pursuit of PhD a significant booster for good and consistent writing?
***
***
PhD programs generally (though not always) offer a lot of flexibility. You can do whatever you want during that time. You can languish for several years and do nothing. You can do a lot of research and crank out academic publications. Or you can do what I did and do enough to write your dissertation while focusing on side hustles and public writing. But if public writing is your goal, I would recommend against getting a PhD, even for the signaling value to impress an audience. If you really need or want some kind of credential, just get a masters. Or just write. If its good, and you share your work often, eventually you will attract an audience.

My sense is that a surprisingly large number of successful substackers have PhDs, but the success didnt come from the PhD. Rather, the kind of person who is disciplined enough to use unstructured time to complete the tasks required for a PhD is also the kind of person who can write regularly for a public audience. Self-discipline. Something I picked up from the military before I went to college.

A question asked on the Google form. Lylian asks: Hi, Rob! Love your work and looking forward to finishing your book. I tried accessing through the thread, but it's only for paid subscribers. Anyway, here's my question: I've known a number of individuals both directly from my own life and through public figures on social media who ended up being adopted as older children by loving families. They're all financially capable--and, seemingly, also emotionally capable--of "paying it forward," but choose to have only biological children when they start their own families. Despite the bureaucratic headache of adoption agencies and/or wanting to breed for narcissistic reasons, I always found that decision befuddling since they are intimately aware of the suffering foster kids endure. Can you shed some light on why this might be and do you plan on adopting if you start your own family in the future? Thank you for your time!

There could be an evolutionary/biological impulse for people to raise their own children. Look up the cinderella effect and youll see that children who live with unrelated adults face a larger likelihood of neglect and abuse. Relatedly, religious communities in the U.S. have historically been more open to adoption, as a result of the belief that we are all Gods children and, perhaps, an associated view that childrearing alone is responsible for differences between kids. I vaguely recall reading a study indicating that, on average, children who are adopted do not attain the same level of education and earnings as their adoptive parents. This is because children who are adopted come from families that are more likely to have attributes associated with interpersonal difficulty, low impulse control, etc. And parents who adopt must meet a certain threshold of income, stability, and so on. My story is particularly unique in that I exceeded my adoptive family in that regard. But in my book I emphasize that conventional success is not the right goal  that childhood well being and flourishing is important in and of itself, independent of how successful they become in the future.
