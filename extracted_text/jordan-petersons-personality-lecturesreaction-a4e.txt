Extraversion is associated with positive emotion; Neuroticism is associated with negative emotion
Roughly, the differences between people who are high vs. low on Neuroticism: Worrying vs. calm, nervous vs. at ease, high-strung vs. relaxed, insecure vs. secure, self-pitying vs. self-satisfied, vulnerable vs. hardy.
Generally speaking, were more sensitive to negative emotion than positive emotion. The magnitude of negative emotion people feel after a loss is higher than the magnitude of positive emotion they feel after an equivalent gain.
The more extraverted someone is, the more oriented they are toward achieving gains. In contrast, highly neurotic people are more oriented toward avoiding losses. Extraversion is associated more with risk-taking, whereas Neuroticism is linked to risk aversion.
Neuroticism can be statistically separated into two aspects: withdrawal and volatility.
Withdrawal is associated with the animal behavior of freezing. Animals freeze because they feel they might be detected by a predator. Its also associated with intense physiological response to danger. A frozen animal, for example, is prepared for danger and thus its heart rate rises, its blood pressure increases, and its muscles tense up.
People who are high on withdrawal often feel blue, are filled with doubts, feel uncomfortable with themselves, are self-conscious, feel threatened easy, often experience low mood, worry about things, easily discouraged, easily embarrassed, overwhelmed by events, and often feel afraid.
Self-consciousness is tightly linked with negative emotion. [This is an interesting term, because self-consciousness means being conscious of others attention on you. You dont feel self-conscious when youre alone; the feeling only arises when others see you and you believe them to be concentrating on your flaws.]
[By the way, this is why its generally a bad idea to be overly late when youre meeting people, especially for the first time. When you keep people waiting, they begin to think of all your flaws.]
Volatility is associated with defensive aggression, which involves warding off attempts to injure or harm you. Volatile people are touchy. They are overresponsive to negative comments. An example of this is road rage. Some people are calm when they drive. Others hurl insults at the drivers around them.
People who are high on volatility get angry easily, often get irritated, get upset easily, have difficulty keeping emotions under control, have frequent mood changes, often lose their composure, are easily annoyed and agitated, and can be stirred up easily. Hyperreactive to frustration, disappointment, and pain.
[In basic training, the instructors assessed new recruits on what they called military bearing, which was essentially about never losing composure or being reactive to setbacks or to being yelled at or disciplined. We just had to stoically accept it. Being reactive would just be met with more yelling and discipline and additional unpleasant tasks. The late teens and early twenties are the period when young males are most likely to be highly volatile. The military wants to screen out males who are unable to control their temperament while also instilling the value of composure to those who are able to be modified by structural incentives.]
In most cases, relationships are better off if both individuals are relatively matched in personality, but Neuroticism is an exception. Its generally better for a person high in Neuroticism to have a partner who is low in Neuroticism.
The best personality trait predictor of divorce is Neuroticism. If both people are high on this trait, divorce is highly likely. Neuroticism is the best predictor of unhappiness in general.
Jordan discusses some of the various negative emotions. Pain and anxiety are separable systems. We feel anxiety in response to the possibility of pain, and the feeling helps us to avoid it. We feel anxiety so that we dont have to feel pain. Of course, anxiety is not pleasant, but its usually better than pain.
Still, pain is fleeting (though typically more damaging than anxiety). In contrast, we feel anxiety when we could potentially be hurt, and we could potentially be hurt all the time. Thus anxiety is less immediately dangerous than pain but more prolonged. Evolution decided its better for us to feel constant low-level anxiety rather than sharp instances of pain.
Jordan: There are a lot more people than you think who are anxious all the time.
[In one of mystudiesfrom grad school, I found that people high on social anxiety rated moral transgressions to be especially objectionable. That is, people who are easily embarrassed and self-conscious also tend to rate conventional moral violations (e.g., shoplifting, cutting in line, insulting others, etc.) as extremely wrong. Jordan found this interesting and retweeted it shortly after it was published.]

Jordan notes that if you are frustrated, disappointed, and grieving, opiates will work to alleviate these feelings. He suggests this is one reason for the opioid epidemic in the U.S.
Antidepressants often work for the alleviation of chronic pain and high levels of stress. To simplify, antidepressants work by increasing the availability of serotonin in the brain, and serotonin is associated with reduced sensitivity to pain and negative emotion. Rising in a hierarchy gives rise to increased levels of serotonin.
Relatedly, people who are higher in a hierarchy are less susceptible to negative emotion because they are more secure and less likely to lose everything and die. In contrast, people at or near the bottom feel more negative emotion because all it takes is one catastrophe for them to be completely dispossessed.
By increasing your brains access to higher levels of serotonin, antidepressants mimic the feeling of rising upward in a hierarchy, and subsequently mitigates negative emotion.
Jordan suggests that antidepressants should be used as a last resort measure for people with low mood. For his clients, hed first check whether they were doing well in their physical health, their careers, their economic situations, their social lives, their romantic interests, and so on. Hed see if any of these domains were the root of his clients unhappiness. And only if everything seemed to be going relatively well, would he then refer them to a physician for medication. If peoples emotions werent aligned with their circumstances, then Jordan would suggest the possibility of antidepressants.
He notes how some of his clients seemed to have everything going wrong for them. Jobless, poor health, little social life, no romantic partner, and so on. Jordan would recommend improvement in these domains first before intervening with medication, unless it was absolutely necessary to forestall the possibility of suicide.
There is some dispute about whether antidepressants work; for some they work well and for others they have little to no effect. Jordan suggests one possible reason why is that for some people, if they have an appalling life, medication isnt going to make them feel better about it. For others, though, who have relatively good lives but whose emotions arent responding appropriately (i.e., they are actually depressed in the clinical sense), antidepressants might actually be an effective treatment.
The pain-related emotions associated with frustration, disappointment, and damage tend to elicit anger and aggression. In contrast, anxiety-related emotions elicit freezing.
One thing that determines negative emotion is how cluttered up the pathway is to your goal.
[Negative emotion arises as a result of increased distance from your goal, positive emotion signals the closing of this distance.]
Jordan notes how he has been jibed for telling people to clean their rooms. But visibly cluttered environments tend to elicit negative emotion, which is why he gives the advice to clean your surroundings and make them beautiful.
Movement toward a goal releases dopamine, which is analgesic (pain relieving). This is why, Jordan says, one of the things a depressed person should do is get moving. Movement to a goal can mitigate pain.
Drugs that give rise to positive emotion also alleviate pain. This includes cocaine and amphetamines.
If someone has intractable pain from cancer, the best combination of drugs is opiates and psycho-motor stimulants like cocaine and heroin. Doctors wont prescribe those, so you can use ADHD drugs instead, in combination with opiates. If you give someone lots of opiates, they can become so sedated that they can no longer function. But if you cut it with Ritalin or some other amphetamine, this will raise positive emotion and boost alertness and activity.
With young children, you can already see marked differences between them in their levels of anxiety, their soothability, their ability to tolerate frustration and disappointment, and their proclivity to withdraw in social situations.
Physical proximity and close contact is also analgesic (pain relieving). This is why when someone is sad, others will hug them or pat them. Jordan points out that this is one reason why a good recommendation for someone who is in pain is to get a massage, because physical touch can relieve their suffering.
[This reminds me of psychologist Harry Harlows experiments on monkeys. In the 1950s (when experimental ethics were less exacting), Harlow and his colleagues performed a series of studies on monkeys. In one study, he created a surrogate mother made of wire and wood and another made of cloth. Infant monkeys were isolated and placed into a cage with these two surrogate mothers. The monkeys overwhelmingly preferred to spend their time clinging to the cloth mother. Even when only the wire mother was equipped with milk, the monkeys only visited to feed, and then return to cling to the cloth mother. Harlow concluded that there is more to the mother-infant bond than milk or nutritional sustenance, and that contact comfort was essential for healthy development.]
[Relatedly, this reminds me of the claims that all that matters is economic or financial circumstances for children. If a kid only has one parent, the logic goes, the real issue is that the single-parent family doesnt have enough money. As if material possessions can replace the emotional and attentive care supplied by having two loving parents present and available. Claiming that money rather than parenting is the real issue for children sounds to me like Let them have wire mothers.]
Babies will actually die without physical contact. Even if given enough food, and nutrition, and shelter, if they dont receive physical touch, their gastrointestinal systems shut down and theyll die.
When kids play, this is an indication that they are comfortable. Kids who are disturbed or anxious will freeze or behave fearfully, but when they are comfortable and feel secure, they will play. If kids are hungry or frightened or otherwise dont have their needs met, they wont play. This is one way you can tell whether your house is well-ordered and emotionally stableif your kids are playing. This indicates that everything else they might need has been taken care of.
[I strongly recommend watching some of the videos documenting Harlows monkey experiments to understand the importance of secure attachment, emotional care, and physical touch]:
Neuroticism has high reliability, psychometrically speaking.
[This means that if you score highly on Neuroticism at age 20, youll tend to score highly at age 60. In psychometrics, this is described as rank-order stability. Its worth clarifying something here. In this hypothetical scenario, as a 60 year old, youll probably score higher than most other 60 year olds, but youll score lower than your 20 year old self. Neuroticism declines, on average, by about 1 standard deviation between age 20 and age 60. So your personality changes with age such that you become less neurotic, but this is true for everyone, so youll be relatively more neurotic than most people your own age but youll be less neurotic than you were as a young adult.]
One reason people experience anxiety is that they have no goal to move toward and thus have little ability to experience positive emotion. Rememberpositive emotion is the result of movement toward a goal. This is why you might be anxious when you have a job but jobless people tend to be most anxious of all.
[Jordan doesnt discuss how modern technology can hijack this emotional system. For some jobless people, a goal might be to get to level 12 or whatever on their world of warcraft game, or getting through an entire season of their favorite anime series, which then gives them some temporary emotional boost. If youre anxious and low in conscientiousness, or if youre avoiding an important task, a good approach is to reduce your ambition to something you will actually do. Dont plan to clean your whole closetyou wont do it. Instead, tell yourself that today you will remove two socks and place them into the dirty laundry bin. Then do the same tomorrow. Build on that habit.
If you still wont do the task, aim even lower. Accept that it may even be humiliating how low you have to aim. If you cant even bear to pick up two socks, try to pick up just one. Any movement forward is better than zero.
[Even if youre relatively high on conscientiousness, this is a useful method. When I signed my book contract, I recall being paralyzed upon realizing what Id actually done. Then I broke the task into small goals. Dont even think about the book. I wasnt writing a book. I was writing 100-500 words a day. I was writing 2,000-2,500 words a week. I was writing a chapter a month. After 18 months of concentrated effort, putting one foot in front of the other, there was a fully formed product called a book manuscript. Same with my doctoral thesis.]
The right hemisphere of the brain is responsible for registering and responding to threat and for global pattern recognition. Jordan gives the example of being home alone watching a horror movie. Youre on edge. Your right hemisphere is active and alert. Suddenly you hear a weird rattling noise in a nearby room. Your imagination can run wild with possibilities for what might have caused that weird noise. The right hemisphere is trying to predict what the source might be.
Everyone has real reason to be anxious and experience pain-related emotions. Were all going to be damaged in one way or another. Also true, though, is that people have an incredible ability to overcome what they face. Once people implement small-scale, voluntary, approach-related changes. Jordan: My conclusion as a clinical psychologist is that as terrible and as paralyzing our propensity for negative emotion is, our ability to overcome it is even stronger.
