What counts as a moral transgression shifts over time. Its worth understanding why this happens.
Digging through the World Values Survey, I found that over time, young people in the U.S. have become more okay with breaking the law.

Controlling for income, education, and political orientation, in 2019, U.S. adults aged 18-25 rated corruption, bribery, and theft as significantly more acceptable compared to decades passed (this was also true without controlling for those variables, but I figured they were obvious possible confounds). My guess is, after 2019, their ratings of permissibility for such activities would be even higher.
Young people today are more lenient about some kinds of immoral actions like crime.
Yet young people are the most supportive of cancel culturea moral movement.

This presents a puzzle.
On the one hand, non-judgmentalism has prevailed because young people are more relaxed about breaking the law.
On the other hand, they are highly judgmental about other acts.
In his 1895 bookThe Rules of the Sociological Method, the sociologist Emile Durkheim suggested that communities have a standard allotment of punishment they mete out for deviant behavior.

There seems to be a law of moral conservation. When societies relax their moral attitudes about some transgressions, people fill the void with new ones.
In other words, we keep the level of moral condemnation the same by redefining what is considered immoral.
Durkheim says there is no such thing as a crime-free community. Even in the most enlightened and nonjudgmental communities, you will find that there are severe punishments for violating certain taboos.
Durkheim writes:
Imagine a society of saintsCrimes will there be unknown; but faults which appear venial to the layman will create there the same scandal that ordinary offense does in ordinary consciousness. If, then, this society has the power to judge and punish, it will define these acts as criminal and will treat them as such.

Durkheim suggests that for misbehavior, as with other social goods, there is a problem of demand exceeding supply.
Why would there be a demand for immoral acts?
Some individuals enjoy broadcasting the misbehavior of others. If all behavior is declared to be okay, people will launch new moral crusades.
During the Cultural Revolution in China, the communists declared that all laws were abolished. Because laws were relics of reactionary and bourgeois styles of thinking.
Interestingly, though, Communists were still throwing people in prisons, shipping them off to labor camps, and executing them by the millions.
They abolished laws. Yet their thirst for punishment did not cease. It grew.
Why cant we have a completely crime free community?
One reason is that some people are simply criminally-inclined. They enjoy breaking rules.
Another less obvious reason, though, is that the definition of what counts as a moral transgression will shift to protect allies and target adversaries.
Some people use morality as a weapon to bolster their own reputations and undercut competitors.
People are resourceful. If you strip them of their old moral weapons, they will find new ones. They will launch new taboos so that they can accuse others of violating them.

Is there evidence that people use morality as a weapon?

To measure moral grandstanding, people rated how much they agree with statements such as:
When I share my moral/political beliefs, I do so to show people who disagree with me that I am better than them and I want to be on the right side of history about moral/political issues.
Participants also reported their status-seeking motives, rating the extent to which they agreed with statements including:
Its important to me that other people look up to me and Its important to me that others respect my rank or position.
To measure political/moral conflict, people rated how often they had experiences such as:
Experienced conflict in my home because of moral/political issues and Gotten into fights on social media because of the political/moral beliefs of others.
In short, moral grandstanders are highly interested in obtaining social status and likely to experience conflicts about political and moral issues.

For moral grandstanders, the aim is power. The specific ideology is unimportant.
If they sense they can use ideological values to obtain power, they will. Theyll accuse others of violating ideological taboos. Or not adhering to the ideology strongly enough.
During the Cultural Revolution, Red Guards would often quote Chairman Maos Little Red Book to justify their actions. But the quotes were contradictory. And inconsistently invoked, depending on context and opportunities to seize power or crush rivals.
Sometimes theyd invoke a quote from Mao saying that mercy is necessary to recruit sympathetic supporters.
Sometimes theyd invoke a quote about how all enemies must be eradicated to advance the aims of the socialist revolution.
Lots of quotes. Lots of ways to accuse others of failing to meet their revolutionary duties.
Lots of ways to bolster ones standing, undercut rivals, and attain power.
Intriguingly, grandstanders are more likely than average to endorse both left-wing authoritarian values and right-wing authoritarian values.
Moral grandstanders tend to agree with left-wing authoritarian statements like:
Our country desperately needs a mighty and liberal leader who will do what has to be done to destroy the radical traditional ways of doing things that are ruining us.
And right-wing authoritarian statements like:
The only way our country can get through the crisis ahead is to get back to our traditional values, put some tough leaders in power, and silence the troublemakers spreading bad ideas.
