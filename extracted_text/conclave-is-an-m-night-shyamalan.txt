"Conclave is an ambitious film covering the secretive world of Vatican politics.
The film is directed by Edward Berger but given the strong start and weak twist ending, it feels more like a high-toned M. Night Shyamalan Movie.
It stars Ralph Fiennes (which I recently learned is pronounced Rayf Fines) as Cardinal Thomas Lawrence, the Dean of the College of Cardinals.
The movie trailer sets you up to expect a gripping exploration of faith, power, and the intricacies of how a new pope is elected.
The story begins with the sudden death of a beloved pope. This plunges the Catholic Church into a state of uncertainty. The world's cardinals convene at the Vatican to vote for his successor.
Fiennes is superb as always. He delivers a subtle performance as Cardinal Lawrence, a man besieged by doubtnot just in his faith, but also in his own ability to manage what is poised to be a contentious conclave (which literally means a private assembly of cardinals for the election of a pope). You learn that shortly before the late pope died, Cardinal Lawrence (Ralph Fiennes) asked him to resign as Dean, depicting Lawrence as a man who has no desire to perform his obligation in organizing the vote for the new pope but does so out of a sense of duty.
Among the leading candidates for the new pope:
Cardinal Bellini, an American liberal who demurs when Lawrence and others ask him if he wants to be the pope. Despite his protestations, clearly harbors ambitions for the papacy.
Cardinal Tedesco, an Italian reactionary yearning for a return to pre-Vatican II practices like the Latin liturgy.
Cardinal Adeyemi, a Nigerian conservative with staunchly traditional views who believes that homosexuals should be imprisoned in this life and burn in hell in the next
And John Lithgow plays Cardinal Tremblay, a Canadian moderate who orchestrates the elimination of his rival Adeyemi by resurfacing Adeyemis sexual indiscretions decades prior with a young woman.
Obviously the best part of the film is watching these people pretend they dont want power while doing all they can to undermine one another to seize the throne.
One of the film's strengths lies in its ability to blend tradition, history, and modernity. Scenes of cardinals navigating the grandeur of the Vatican are juxtaposed with moments of mundane modern reality. Cell phones buzz. Security machines beep. You see cardinals vaping over lunch.
Still, "Conclave" fails in its execution due to a twist ending that feels both abrupt and unearned.
Early in the film, you see the arrival of Archbishop Vincent Benitez, a latecomer to the conclave claiming to have been secretly appointed by the deceased pope. There is an element of mystery surrounding the character. Hes Mexican but performs missionary work in war zones in Afghanistan.
Heres the big spoiler:
Benitez is intersex. Benitez has female body parts but presents and lives as a man. He is then elected as Pope Innocent XIV.
This out-of-the-blue twist comes without sufficient foreshadowing.
The lack of narrative breadcrumbs leaves the audience feeling blindsided.
The film attempts to tackle themes of acceptance and the evolving nature of the Church, but the twist seems more like a contrivance than a natural progression. In the context of the film it makes no sense at all. Within the text it makes no sense, but you as the viewer have extratextual informationthe chattering class is preoccupied with gender bending and trans issues and so this film was made as Oscar-bait to please film critics.
Anyway. Effective storytelling, especially in a suspense or a thriller, relies on subtle hints and developments that lead to a satisfying conclusion. In this case, the absence of such groundwork renders the climax hollow.
Moreover, the movie introduces intriguing subplots and character dynamics that are left unexplored.
An Islamic suicide bomber attacks the Vatican, killing dozens and damaging the Sistine Chapel.
Cardinal Tedesco, an Italian reactionary gunning for the papacy, delivers a rousing speech about unity and tradition and describes terrorists as animals. In another scene, during dinner, he accurately points out that the other cardinals are seated with fellow countrymen who speak the same language with one another (French, Italian, etc.). Tedesco says a unifying language binds different people more closely together under a shared purpose. He laments the death of the Latin language.
After Tedesco calls the terrorists animals, the intersex trans Cardinal Benitez steps in and preaches tolerance and is subsequently voted into the papacy by the other cardinals.
Interestingly, early in the film, you see one of the cardinals observe that the Nigerian cardinal Adeyemi (who believes homosexuals should be imprisoned) is more reactionary than Cardinal Tedesco, but because Adeyemi is not white he gets a pass for his extreme views.
Ralph Fiennes anchors the film with his portrayal of Cardinal Lawrence. His internal struggle adds depth to the movie. But his character arc feels incomplete due to the film's weird ending.
"Conclave" is a film that starts as an A-minus as you watch the political intrigue and cloak and dagger methods the cardinals use in their quest to be elected pope by their fellow cardinals. But due to the dumb twist, it slips to a D-plus.
You have to give the audience breadcrumbs for the ending to feel satisfying otherwise you come away feeling cheated.
Early in the film, Bellini tells Lawrence that the late pope was "always eight moves ahead," suggesting the late pope was a master strategist with a grand plan. But this foreshadowing leads nowhere, as the popes alleged brilliance isnt demonstrated in the plot.
In contrast, there is zero foreshadowing for the unexpected revelation about Cardinal Benitez being trans/intersex/whatever. The film hints at a cunning plan by the pope that never materializes (foreshadowing that goes nowhere) while failing to foreshadow the significant surprise at the end. I cant stress how much the ending blindsides you if you dont know what to expect in advance.
The movie sits at 93% with critics on Rotten Tomatoes (this makes sense). Somehow it has 86% with the audience (this doesnt make sense).
If you want to watch an equally strong Ralph Fiennes performance in a film with a far better plot (and plot twist), I recommend checking out The Menu (2022).
