Back during the lockdowns of 2020, I took a long walk and listened to an episode of The Portal podcast where Eric Weinstein interviewed J.D. Vance.
J.D. remarked on the benefits of having extended family around to raise children. At one point, Eric, a mathematician, says the whole purpose of the postmenopausal female in theory is to help with childcare. I listened to this and, in the midst of my psychology PhD program where I was steeped in evolutionary psychology research, thought nothing of it. It was perhaps a bit overstated, but not untrue, according to a large body of empirical and theoretical research.

Anyway, its worth exploring what Eric Weinstein meant by his remarks regarding menopause.
We humans are unique among our primate cousins in the female tendency to outlive fertility. For example, the latest that chimpanzee females tend to give birth is around age 40, and the longest they tend to live is also around age 40. In contrast, the latest that women tend to give birth is around age 40, yet even in hunter-gatherer societies women often live beyond the age of 70 (and women across societies tend to outlive men).
This has given risethe grandmother hypothesis: the idea that women evolved such a long post-menopausal lifespan because grandparental investment in the form of help, care, food, and wisdom enabled young women to have more offspring.

Our ancestors were more likely to survive childhood and mothers were more likely to have a larger number of children if they had the help of a grandmother. Evolution created grandmothers by inhibiting womens ability to reproduce after a certain age while they still had many years of life ahead of them. This seems to be why human females evolved menopause.
A related idea is the absent father hypothesis, or the idea that because men tend to die younger than women (and if men live, they sometimes desert their partners for a younger woman), it would have been beneficial for women to have assistance from their mothers to help with childcare. Interestingly, evidence suggests that grandmothers do have beneficial effects on grandchildren, especially under harsh or unstable circumstances.

During this period in Finland (and everywhere else on earth), illnesses, infections, and injuries resulted in a childhood mortality rate of about 50%. The presence of grandmothers made a large difference on whether a kid reached adulthood or not.
Compared with paternal grandparents, maternal grandparents tend to invest more in their grandchildren. On average, maternal grandparents spend more time with their grandchildren and devote more resources to them, relative to paternal grandparents.
The logic here is that just as mothers are always more confident than fathers that their offspring are truly theirs, maternal grandparents are more certain that the grandchildren are genetically related to them. There is no question whether their daughters child is their blood relative. In contrast, throughout evolutionary history, paternal grandparents would have less confidence that their sons children were truly related to them.

A meta-analysis of 17 studies alsorevealedthat the presence of maternal grandparents increased the survival rates of children.

Often these evolutionary mechanisms apply even when the original reason for their development is absent.
When I think back to my own childhood, even before my adoptive parents got divorced, I still felt closer with my maternal grandparents than my paternal ones. The parents of my adoptive father were nice enough. They bought me a Playstation for my birthday. But the parents of my adoptive mother, though much poorer, were more involved in my life. My adoptive moms mother (who I called grandma) would spend hours telling me stories or asking me questions about my life. I havent seen any studies on the question of whether the maternal grandparents of adopted children invest more in them, but I would guess the answer is yes.
J.D. himself knows something about the grandmother hypothesis. He dedicates a large portion of his bookHillbilly Elegyto describing the benefits of being raised for a time by a woman he called mamaw, his moms mother. After living with his drug addict mom and her whirlwind of different boyfriends, J.D. eventually moves in with his maternal grandparents, who he credits with providing some semblance of stability in his early life.

I know that the way Eric Weinstein phrased his language could strike people as insulting. But its a freewheeling podcast where people speak openly. Moreover, just because we evolved certain evolutionary adaptations for specific purposes does not mean that we must adhere to those purposes or refrain from using these adaptations for other goals.
For example, noses evolved to smell. But now you use your nose to hold up your Ray-Bans. If someone tells you that the whole purpose of your nose is to smell, hes not telling you to ditch your shades.
I wish we could all grow the fuck up. But its an election year. So here we are.
