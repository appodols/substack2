In her excellent 2023 bookEve: How the Female Body Drove 200 Million Years of Human Evolution,Cat Bohannon points out that promiscuity has long been a strategic behavior in many primate species. Among our closest relativeschimpanzees, bonobos, and orangutansfemales engage in it for a specific purpose. It's not just about pleasure; it's a deliberate way to reduce the possibility of infanticide, as no local male can be certain of paternity. The more males who believed they might be the father of an infant, the safer that infant was likely to be.

An explanation about why (some) women are intrigued by and attracted to violent men recently occurred to me. This explanation came to mind after reading texts by the primatologist Frans de Waal, the evolutionary biologist Richard Wrangham, and other papers and books, as well as closely watching documentaries about chimpanzee troupes and gorilla harems. I havent seen a strong evolutionary explanation for womens attraction to dark triad personalities, so here is my contribution:
The last common ancestor we shared with our nearest evolutionary relatives did not have the same mating patterns as modern humans. It is more plausible that their behaviors were similar to those of our current primate cousins.
Our closest relatives, chimpanzees and gorillas, have one notable behavior in common: they engage ina lotof infanticide. Data from primatologist Diane Fossey suggest that about 1 in 7 gorilla infants is killed by a male gorilla. This rate is high enough that the average female gorilla is more likely than not to experience the loss of an infant in her lifetime. When a gorilla infant is killed by a male, the infant's mother often becomes the partner of the killer male. The logic here is that if she partners with this violent male, he is unlikely to kill his own offspring (but would kill her offspring that resulted from other pairings).

Infanticide also occurs among chimpanzees. Interestingly, pregnant chimpanzee females often leave the group to give birth to reduce any risk to her or her newborn. Chimp females are highly promiscuous. They seek out multiple partners during their fertile period each month. This behavior serves to obscure paternity. This reduces the odds that any male will kill her offspring, as each male believes he could be the father.
Moreover, female chimps are highly likely to mate with the most aggressive males. And the males who are the most aggressive toward females tend to have the most offspring. Some of this involves coercion, though many female chimpanzees appear to "willingly" go along with these violent males, suggesting that such male behavior leads the females to experience what humans might describe as sexual arousal.
Chimp males commonly beat up on females, often in surprise attacks. A grim passage from Wrangham's "The Goodness Paradox":
One attack at Kanyawara, in western Ugandalasted for a full eight minutes, during which a male grabbed sticks and beat the female intermittently with them, when he was not slapping, punching, and kicking her. The males aim in such attacks is to intimidate the chosen female into readily acceding to his future demands for sex. For each female, one male distinguishes himself from other males by being the one who most frequently attacks her. The tactic is often successful. Over subsequent weeks, a females most frequent aggressor tends to be her most frequent sex partner, and eventually, even though she is likely to mate several times with every male in her community, he will be the most likely father of her next baby. This stomach-churching practice is part of the reason why, as males become adult, they go through a ritual of beating up on every female. A males ability to intimidate females is a vital component of his strategy for having as many offspring as possible.

If a female detects an unusually aggressive and threatening male in her surroundings, evolutionary logic suggests mating with him would be adaptive (in an evolutionary sense).
My suggestion is that the attraction to violent males is an adaptive strategy to reduce offspring mortality. This impulse is present not just in our great ape cousins, but in ourselves as well. Arousal in response to a violent male reduces the likelihood of infanticide. For our ancestors, it increased the likelihood of survival and reproduction. In other words, the protective strategies that existed in our nearest common ape ancestors (and is still practiced by chimps and gorillas today) persist in modern human females.
These evolutionary impulses are expressed in various and sometimes perplexing ways in the modern world. Letters to serial killers and so forth.

Whether a behavior evolved because it was directly selected for, or because it was a by-product of another adaptive feature, or indeed, whether it evolved at all, should not color our moral judgments. Many tendencies that we regard as morally reprehensible clearly evolved, including numerous kinds of sexual coercion, lethal violence, and social domination. Equally, many morally delightful tendencies did not evolve, such as charity to strangers and kindness to animals. Our decisions about which behaviors we like or dislike should never be attributed to our understanding of their evolutionary history or adaptive value.
