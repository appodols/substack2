One of my assumptions about the partisan divide in the country follows quite a bit along what you said w.r.t. blue-collar voters, and it goes back to some pretty basic philosophy: am I who I am primarily based on my (verbal, propositional) thoughts, or am I who I am based on how I feel. And it seems to me that the educational (and media/progressive) elite has become fairly detached from reality where hard-to-express (pre-verbal) experiences about the world come into play. Similar to Thomas Sowell's argument about the Anointed, the left-progressive worldview is based on abstract and idealized rules about the (human and social) world, and each time when some basic feelings (borders matter, and fetuses are potential humans in the making, who need protections, etc.) collide with that worldview, the verbally high-intelligent and educated people have a tendency of wanting to override those feelings (of the unwashed masses) with some propositionally justifiable (but fairly dogmatic) belief system, which in the end always turns out to be a house of cards. Trump, on the other hand, firmly comes from a "shooting from the emotional hip" place. He reads the cultural room and moment, and then promulgates a position that speaks to how people feel, deep down, about issues. As a salesman, he is really, really good at that, and because he speaks to A LOT of people's unspoken (and hard to talk about in polite society) feelings, his support (in polling and elsewhere) is probably often underestimated, because when you ask people why they vote for Trump, it comes out (in the ears of the propositionally high minded, anyway) as some version of "because!"
