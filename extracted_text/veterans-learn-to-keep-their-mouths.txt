When I arrived at Yale in 2015, I witnessed the birth of a new form of radical campus activism that would soon overflow the boundaries of the university; what people today refer to as wokeness. And then I heard the refrain that today has become something of a cliche: Its just a few college kids.
Kids. A huge segment of this country faces the full brunt of reality at 18 or even earlier. But if you go to college and then grad school at the richest universities on earth, you get to be a kid until your 27th birthday.

A fellow military veteran asked me this question a couple of years ago, when I was an undergradat Yale. Like me, he had recently transitioned out of the military and was studying at another university.
He said he was mystified, observing that working class people in the military swear an oath to defend with their lives the U.S. constitution, including the first and second amendments. Meanwhile, affluent people in college continually contest the first and seek to dismantle the second. In short, he questioned whether veterans are duped into believing they are upholding American values while the richest kids in the world try to undermine them.
Hes not the only one who has harbored these sentiments. Many veterans I know who have managed to get into college are bewildered by what they see: students from the top income decile expressing derision for the values they signed up to defend. Perhaps they could be forgiven for feeling like suckers.

The purpose is to denigrate a transgressor in order to bond with observers. Its effective.

Still, many of these rich students love American values, too. But in order to distinguish themselves, they rail against those values.
