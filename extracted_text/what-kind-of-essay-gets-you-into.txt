In addition to the critics, the startup kid had his supporters who expressed frustration. There is a sense that this kid, who looks white, would almost certainly have gotten into several of the schools that rejected him had he been nonwhite (or non-Asian).
More and more young men are withdrawing from higher ed. Here you have an ambitious young guy who has already accomplished something and he was rejected from nearly all of the top colleges to which he applied.
No one needs to feel sorry for this young guyhell be fine. The attention his case is getting isnt really about him. People are using his case as a way to vent and point out the inherent unfairness of the admissions process.
The thing that I have not seen anyone point out is that the kid's biggest problem was lack of good advice. With the right guidance, he wouldve written a very different statement and likely been admitted. In that sense, the schools you get into dont reflect who you are but rather who is advising you.

, Northwestern University Professor Lauren Rivera documented hiring practices at top-tier firms. She wrote that employers seek out compelling personal stories, with personal experiences as a series of progressive decisions driven by intrinsic motives and the pursuit of personal passion and meaning.
In other words, the story must be propelled by the candidate. Things cant just happen to youyou have to make things happen. Moreover, adversity isnt enough. Elite employers want to see how such suffering reveals or cultivates desired qualities like resilience, curiosity, or empathy.
Though Rivera wrote about top firms, her observations could easily apply to top universities. Who knows how to tell their stories in the right way?

In high school, I read Down and Out in Paris and London by George Orwell, about his experiences in the slums of France and England. Because I worked as a busboy and dishwasher, I liked his description of how working in a busy restaurant was like racing to sort a deck of cards against the clockthe task is easy, but the time constraints make it hard.
I was later disappointed to learn that when Orwell came from privilege. When he became penniless during the periods covered in Down and Out, his well-to-do family had taken him in.
Later, though, my appreciation of his work was restored. It took someone like Orwell to write about life in the slums in a way that other educated people would care about. In my case, I had to spend time around the upper middle class before I could describe my life in a way they would understand.
In my case, I was extremely unlucky in terms of how I grew up, but was extremely lucky that I later learned how to tell that story in the right way.
I applied to college after spending eight years in the military. Before completing my applications, I was a participant in something called the Warrior-Scholar project, which paired veterans with recent college graduates who served as tutors to help us refresh our academic skills before applying.
I had decided to write about my experiences in foster care and my unstable childhood in my application  if nothing else, it was a chance to explain why my high school grades had been so abysmal  but when I sent drafts of my essays to a tutor, a 22 year old Yale graduate, for feedback, he repeatedly sent them back, urging me to add more color and creativity.
I described how Id lived in various foster homes and later joined the military right after high school. Why? the tutor asked me.
Well, I was desperate. I felt I had nowhere else to go. I wanted to run away and enlisting seemed like the most expedient option. After telling him this, he suggested I use the term initiative. The idea was that I was finally taking control of my life after so many adultsbirth parents, foster parents, social workers, judges, adoptive parents, and so onhad let me down.
It was objectively true. Im the one who signed those enlistment papers, after all. But is that really how I felt when I was 17? I just wanted to get away. It wasnt a reflective decision to take initiative and steer my life in a positive direction. It was more of an impulsive decision escape where I was from.
Gradually, I got the point: I couldnt just relay my life story; I needed to turn it into a narrative. That is, I had to write about how these experiences affected me, what I did about them, what they said about my character. So in addition to initiative, I stressed the theme of resilience. Again, this was true. I had been through a lot in my life. But honestly, I had never thought of myself as an unusually resilient person, because so many of the people Id grown up around had been through similar experiences.
I saw myself as connected with them, rather than somehow separate or unique.
Still, the tutor urged me to describe what was unique about my story, and this was a way to do it. Without that tutor, I would have never gotten there.
Paradoxically, I needed help from someone from a different social class to tell my own story effectively.
Colleges say they use essays in an effort to increase student diversity. The theory goes that essays, in particular, allow applicants the scope to write about how they have overcome hardship and explain how they will contribute to the campus community.
Recall that there are something like 40,000 applicants each year who score at least 1500 on the SAT, and only around 20,000 slots each year for Ivy League colleges. The aim is to use essays to help applicants stand out amid all the undifferentiated academic excellence.
But what if those with the best stories to tell arent equipped to tell them in a way that will resonate with admissions committees?

Ironically, affluent college applicants are the most adept at figuring out that writing a trendy hashtag 100 times in a row is a far better guarantor of admission than writing about the successful company you built or about the adversity youve overcome.
People who have gone through real shit are extremely reluctant to talk about it. Unless they have done a lot of inner work. In which case they rarely cast themselves as a victim.
For many people, if you've truly been through something stressful or traumatic, there's a reluctance to speak about it. You don't want that to be your identity. You want to rise above it by embracing a different aspect of your identity.
You want to talk about your interests or your intellectual preoccupations or the hobbies that allowed you to distract yourself from, or allowed you to draw your attention away from, those negative experiences. And if youre asked to talk about those experiences, you might not know how to put it into words that cause just the right amount of discomfort for the admissions officer. The Disney version of the story rather than the real thing.
Whereas people who have grown up relatively affluent and privileged, and maybe they've experienced some mild adversity, they know how to use the right language and the right jargon and terminology, hitting all those right buttons so that another privileged person can read it and say, Okay, you're the right kind of marginalized, you're the right kind of person who knows how to communicate your story in a compelling way.
Really, what these places want is for you to tell them how an elite education is going to make you a better person. When in reality it often does the opposite.
If elite colleges truly want diversitynot just of background, but of thought and experiencetheyll need to rethink how they evaluate personal stories. That starts with recognizing the hidden curriculum of admissions and asking how many promising students theyre missing simply because no one ever showed them how to speak the language.
