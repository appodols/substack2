Some examples of nonverbal behavior: gazing, touching, hugging, smiling, and grooming
You dont have to say anything in order for these nonverbal behaviors to convey their meaning. Their message is sent with the gesture alone
Some of these behaviors have meaning in one culture and no meaning or a different meaning in another. For example, giving a thumbs up, an okay sign, or a peace sign
Other nonverbal behaviors accompany verbal communication to emphasize or underscore meaning. For example, eyebrow raising or head nods
Though specifics may vary by culture, nonverbal behaviors come naturally to us
Emotional expressions are forms of nonverbal behavior that convey internal states
How can we distinguish between authentic emotional expressions versus inauthentic emotional expressions?
Genuine emotional expressions tend to be relatively fleeting. A genuine smile, for example, typically lasts between 4 to 6 seconds. A social, or feigned, smile can be exceptionally brief, lasting only a fraction of a second or endure for a long period of time, such as for an entire conversation
Authentic emotional expressions involve involuntary muscle actions. People have a hard time expressing these in the absence of genuine emotion, and likewise have a hard time suppressing them when they feel a genuine emotion
Cross-cultural studies suggest that emotional expressions are universally recognized. People in remote, non-western communities recognize and categorize emotions such as sadness, anger, and so on in the same way as westerners
The face reveals a great deal of information and facial expressions are difficult to fake
This raises the question of why? What are the benefits of advertising something as personal, intimate and subjective as our own internal motivational states? Would it not be better to keep our emotional states privatewould a poker face be better? Why did we evolve to exhibit our internal states on something as conspicuous as our faces?

Professor Reed distinguishes between 2 different self-conscious emotions
Exposed self-conscious emotions
Evaluative self-conscious emotions
[Self-conscious emotions such as shyness, embarrassment and shame are all related to how we experience ourselves in the minds of others]
Exposed self-conscious emotions are directly tied to how we perceive ourselves
Examples of exposed self-conscious emotions include embarrassment, jealousy, and empathy
Evaluative self-conscious emotions are about how we evaluate ourselves. These emotions require not only that we perceive ourselves, but also require that we have a standard rule or goal that we have succeeded or failed to uphold or attain
Examples of evaluative self-conscious emotions include shame, pride, and hubris
These emotions are distinct from more basic emotions such as fear, which does not require any real sense of self-consciousness. Researchers have found that infants can feel fear (which is not a self-reflective emotion), but not embarrassment (which is)
Embarrassment is a feeling that involves discrediting ones own image in the eyes of others
There are 3 characteristic non-verbal behaviors associated with embarrassment:
Smile
Inverted gaze; avoidance of eye contact with others
Movement of your hands to your body; touching your face, your hair, etc.
People who experience embarrassment show different non-verbal behaviors compared with when they feel shame, fear, and anxiety
For instance, smiles associated with embarrassment are sheepish, or silly. In contrast, people who experience shame, anxiety, or fear typically do not smile
When were embarrassed, we tend to tilt our heads and avoid eye contact. In contrast, when people feel shame, they often bow their heads
When embarrassed, people touch their bodies, engaging in a kind of self-soothing behavior but people who feel shame or shyness do not tend to do this
Embarrassment can arise merely from being noticed, even without any negative evaluation
You may have felt embarrassed, for example, upon receiving a compliment. Someone praised you, and you felt embarrassment, an exposed self-conscious emotion. Merely being reminded that you are a body that exists in the social world, even one that is well-regarded, can make you feel embarrassed
Moving onto jealousy; jealousy is triggered by the loss of something valuable. People tend to confuse jealousy with envy, which is triggered by wanting something another person has
In either case, both jealousy and envy require self-reflection. You reflect on yourself and what it is that you dont have
Reed cites a study in which researchers had mothers interact with their 4 month old infants. The mothers then turned away from their infants, ignoring them and interacted with a toy doll. The mothers showed either positive or neutral emotions toward the doll. The researchers measured the infants non-verbal emotional behavior and found that infants displayed the strongest negative emotion when they saw their mothers display positive emotions toward the toy doll. This suggests that infants as young as 4 months old can experience jealousy
Interestingly, kids tend to be freely willing to share their toys with other children until about the age of 2. Then, consciousness begins to develop, and children begin to exhibit more pronounced jealousy and possessiveness and subsequently become more reluctant to share
Reed transitions into discussing empathy. Empathy involves placing yourself into the shoes of another person
Empathy is the ability and tendency to share and understand the internal state of others
Affective empathy involves taking on the emotional states of others. For example, you see a sad person crying, and you yourself feel sad and might even begin to tear up
Cognitive empathy, sometimes called mentalizing, involves taking on the perspective of others. This is distinct from merely feeling what other people feel. Cognitive empathy means to understand that someone walking down a dark alley feels fear, or that a person who just learned of a death in the family feels sad, even if you yourself do not actually feel fear or sadness

Reed covers the topic of theory of mind, sometimes called mentalizing, cognitive empathy, or social intelligence
Theory of mind (social intelligence) is the ability to understand the mental states of others. This ability allows us to guess with some accuracy the thoughts, intentions, and future behavior of other people
Imagine you see your coworker carrying an umbrella. Your theory of mind allows you to know that this person is anticipating that it will rain later, even if you yourself hadnt considered that it might rain
The absence of this ability (theory of mind, or social intelligence) characterizes autism spectrum disorder
Moving onto the evaluative self-conscious emotions, Reed concentrates on pride
Pride is positive emotion we experience when we achieve some pre-determined goal
Pride is distinguished from other positive emotions like happiness, because we are able to experience happiness in response to other situations that we were not responsible for bringing about
If you won the lottery, you might feel happy, perhaps even elated. But you probably wouldnt feel pride, the way you would if you aced an exam or won a gold medal. This is because winning the lottery is based on luck, whereas scoring highly on an exam or winning the gold is based on skill
Hubris is similar to pride. The difference, though, is in what elicits the feeling. Pride comes from success at an event we played a part in achieving; hubris comes from something we have undeservingly given ourselves credit for
Hubris is a self-aggrandizing emotion; one in which people feel good about themselves even if they themselves are not responsible or played no meaningful role in achieving a desirable outcome or good fortune
[One example that comes to mind is a smug trust fund kid who thinks hes superior simply because he was born rich]
Many conceptions of narcissistic personality disorder suggests that narcissists indulge in hubris to counteract feelings of inferiority and/or mediocrity
Shame is another self-conscious evaluative emotion, which can be thought of as the opposite of pride. We feel pride when we achieve a desired goal, whereas shame arises when we recognize our failure to achieve a desirable goal
Shame is a negative and painful experience accompanied by the wish to hide, disappear, or even die
When we feel shame, we lower our heads and wish to be inconspicuous; when we feel pride we expand our chests and bodies and want others to recognize us and our achievements

Both shame and guilt arise from a failure to achieve success

Other people -- for instance the influential Ruth Benedict in The Chyrsanthemum and the Sword -- have a different definition of shame and guilt. They claim they are both about the perception of your behaviour. Guilt is what you feel when you haven't lived up to your own personal standard of behaviour. Your self-regard views you harshly. Shame is what you feel when the neighbours disapprove of your behaviour. Thus you can feel guilty about misbehaviour that the neighbours don't know about, and you believe never will, without feeling shame,  and if you have a particularly demanding standard of personal behviour you can feel guilty about conduct that the neighbours all know about and don't disapprove of.  "You are being too hard on yourself"  they claim.  Contrariwise,  you can feel shame even when you have done something virtuous in your own eyes if you know that the neighbours know and dislike you for it.  Prejudice and tribal loyalty can mean that the the neighbours dislike you simply for  existing as a member of an out-group.  You may feel shame about this, but not guilt.
