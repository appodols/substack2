[In square brackets are thoughts/notes I jotted while listening]:
[Jordan doesnt offer an overall definition of trait Extraversion. Here is a good one Ive read: The degree to which a person needs attention and social interaction.]
Jordan points out that each of the Big Five personality traits can be broken down statistically into two aspects. Extraversion breaks down into assertiveness and enthusiasm.
Some features of assertiveness: Taking charge, having a strong personality, exhibiting a talent for influencing people, knowing how to captivate people, not waiting for others to lead the way, seeing yourself as a good leader, ability to talk others into doing things, unlikely to hold back opinions, being the first to act.
Jordan points out that it is easy to conflate assertiveness with leadership, but they are not precisely the same thing. You might want a leader who is a visionary (high on trait Openness), or reliable (high on Conscientiousness), or calm in a crisis (low on Neuroticism).
Some features of enthusiasm: Making friends easily, being easy to get to know, doesnt keep others at a distance, being willing to reveal details about yourself (Jordan describes highly enthusiastic people he has met whocouldntnottell you everything about themselves), warming up quickly to others, often caught up in excitement, exhibiting feelings when happy, having a lot of fun, laughing a lot.

[Ive seen studies indicating that people are, on average, willing to incur a financial cost in order to express themselves to others when playing economic games in laboratory settings. My guess is that the desire to do this is moderated by personalityextraverts would be more willing to pay a fee to speak their minds, relative to introverts.]
[25% of Twitter users produce 97% of the tweets. These users are probably particularly high on trait Extraversion, relative to other Twitter users. Some people speculate that social media offers a platform for introverts to express themselves. This is probably true to some extent. Overall, though, my guess is that peoples personalities are pretty similar both online and in real life.]
[Btw, this is reflected inresearchon online trolling. A common misconception is that social media turns ordinary nice people into vicious trolls. This doesnt appear to be the case. Rather, people who are hostile offline are also more likely to be hostile online. And because online hostility can be viewed by thousands of people simultaneously, and negativity draws attention, people mistakenly believe everyone turns into a jerk online. In response to jerks being hostile online, regular people decide to post less often or not at all. So jerks post a disproportionate share of content, creating the illusion that there are lots of jerks. Again, one takeaway here is that most people (though not all) are pretty similar online and offline.]

[You can score highly on one aspect and relatively low on another, which is why using the aggregate score on a personality trait can sometimes be misleading. How did you score highly on Extraversion when you seldom take charge/express joy? Usually this means that your score on one of the aspects is noticeably higher than the other.]
These traits are genetically influenced. Most of us have a set point, and can be environmentally moved in one direction or another. But the further you want to move from your setpoint, the harder it becomes. You can go from being a little bit introverted to, with lots of practice and effort, becoming moderately extraverted. But the more extraverted you want to become, the harder it will be.
Its easier for people to choose a job that matches their personality than to adjust their personality to match their job. [Capitalizing on your intrinsic propensities is much easier than trying to modify yourself to the environment. This is why you shouldnt pick your job based on whats popular in the job market. Thats not a good way to be happy in the long run. The right job is one that suits your personality, not one that happens to be in demand right now.]
Figure out what youre like, and find the niche that matches who you are. [If youre moderately extraverted, then choosing a job that requires extreme extraversion might not be a bad idea. You can probably stretch yourself if you need to. But if youre a natural introvert, then being a salesman is probably not a good plan. Go with the grain of your personality, and be willing to step a little out of your comfort zone. But if you go too far, you will likely become unhappy.]
Other features of Extraversion (these describe Extraversion in general rather than solely assertiveness or enthusiasm): sociable, fun-loving, affectionate, friendly, spontaneous, talkative, making eye contact, drawn to sales and personnel work, theyre more impulsive, theyre more likely to gamble, more sexually active, and more likely discount the future.
Jordan suggests people try to chemically modify themselves to adopt some of these traits. Alcohol and certain kinds of drugs facilitate extraversion and its associated features. There can be a link between impulsivity and pleasure.
But too much impulsivity and positive emotion can be dangerous. Bipolar people in their manic phase are often overwhelmed with positive emotion and behave impulsively in the moment, often hurting themselves or others in the process. Or their heads fill with grandiose plans andthey  spend all of their money. Once out of their manic phase, they find themselves in serious trouble.
Jordan criticizes the idea that we should always be happy. Rather, your happiness should reflect the reality of your current circumstances. [Emotions, both positive and negative, are useful information. Thinking about how unhappy I was as a kid, if someone had given me a pill that induced permanent happiness, I probably would have never acted to improve my life.]
Jordan describes research indicating that people show a stronger preference for being free of pain and anxiety than they do for happiness. Rather than desire positive emotion, people seem to want to be free of negative emotion. Measures of well-being correlate more strongly with trait Neuroticism (which encompasses anxiety) rather than Extraversion (which is more closely aligned with conventional notions of happiness).
[This is generally consistent with the Kahneman and Tverskys prospect theory, one principle of which is that losses loom larger than gains. Losing a dollar is twice as painful as gaining a dollar is pleasurable (I know some of this work has been challenged but the basic result has remained intact). For most people, not being poor is more desirable than being rich.]
When you feel anxious and frustrated, thats not only psychological. There are underlying physiological manifestations of these negative emotions. These emotions activate an emergency response system in your body to prepare for what might happen, which takes a toll on you mentally and physically. Unstable environments are inherently unpleasant.
Jordan draws an analogy to being in a car with your feet on both the gas and the brake. You are prepared for anything, which is very hard on the vehicle (your body). Uncertainty puts you in a state of hyper-preparation, which leads you to burn up resources ordinarily reserved for the future. This makes you age faster, weakens your immune system, and increases the likelihood of illness.
[Relatedly, there isresearchindicating that people with adverse childhood experiencesphysical or emotional abuse, neglect, poverty, parental divorce, and so onage faster. Children with stressful lives tend to get their adult teeth earlier, reach puberty sooner, and undergo accelerated changes in their brain structure. This also implies that a very comfortable upbringing slows maturation. In high school, the guys I grew up with looked older than many of my college peers.]

Jordan supplies some advice for relationship disputes: The aim is to find a solution, not to argue more (assuming you are not a toxic person or with a toxic person or in a toxic relationship). Ask yourself, what is the minimum precondition for satisfaction? When you are in the midst of an argument or disagreement, ask, what is the smallest act your partner could do to satisfy you? And make the request. And be willing to do the same for your partner when he or she makes a request of you.
Positive emotion comes from moving towards your goals. That is their function. [This is why drugs can be so dangerous. They deliver positive emotions without having to actually achieve anything. The same could be said for porn and video games.]
The more extraverted you are, the more pleasure youre going to feel as you move closer to your goals. More introverted and inhibited people are going to think, okay, youve had some success, but dont get too excited because you never know what might come next. More extraverted people are going to think, this is great, my plan is working. People who are manic or grandiose are on the extreme end of this and think they can do no wrong.
Jordan describes the brain hemispheres and how the left hemisphere governs positive emotion and movement forward and the right hemisphere governs negative emotion and uncertainty.
People who are depressed exhibit more action in their brains right hemisphere.
The left hemisphere (which governs positive emotion) also specializes in language for most people. People who are extraverted tend to be more verbally fluent and more talkative. People also talk more when theyre happy and less when theyre sad. There is a tight link between sociability and talking and happiness. [This is likely why one of the strongest predictors of happiness is the frequency and intensity of social interactions].
Jordan closes off this lecture by saying our personality traits can be thought of as bets. And the gamble extraverted people make is that the worlds a pretty damn good place and that you can succeed by interacting with other people. People high on Extraversion see the social world as full of opportunities.
[Evidence suggests that this bet pays off. Extraversion is associated with higher earnings. Verbal fluency predicts occupational success. I shared some thoughts about how to become a better conversationalisthere.]
