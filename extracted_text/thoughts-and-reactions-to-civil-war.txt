I just finished this movie about an hour ago so here is a very fresh review. I allowed myself some popcorn and a large Coke Zero, so I jotted these down in a well-caffeinated state.
My somewhat tongue-in-cheek take is that the main message of the movie is that young and naive people are dangerous and if not properly restrained and tamed they will get you killed.
Some spoilers ahead.
None of the main characters would have died if Lee (Kirsten Dunst) and Joel hadnt allowed Jessie to come along on the ride with them.Jessie is a young aspiring journalist who admires Lee. Lee and Joel reluctantly agree to allow Jessie to tag along with them from New York to D.C., where they plan to photograph the ongoing war and attempt by the Western Forces to capture/kill the president.
Had Jessie not impulsively jumped into the car with Evan, doing the monkey see monkey do thing after seeing Nelson do it, then Evan and Nelson wouldnt have gotten shot.
Neither would Sammy, the veteran journalist who saved Jessies life before being shot himself.
Lee herself wouldnt have died had Jessie not stupidly moved into the center of the hallway during the White House gunfight.
Weirdly, the movie focuses on Jessies age rather than her lack of experience. At the beginning of the movie, Lee calls her a kid and Joel replies Shes 23 (in real life, the actress is 25-26 according toWikipedia). 23 isnt a kid. Some of those guys with rifles in the movie were younger than Jessie. I get that they wanted to portray her as inexperienced, but they could have focused on that rather than her specific age. In any wartime scenario, most of the shooting and killing is done by people (males, primarily) in their twenties.

Its unclear what the ethnic standards are for what I assume is meant to be an ultranationalist militia. They kill one guy from Hong Kong who has an obvious accent. But Joel has an obvious accent and they spare him. Of course, its also possible that they have very soft standards and they simply kill whenever they feel like killing and then rationalize it after the fact. At a certain point, boundaries become blurred and principles shift in wartime environments.
Lee, Joel, and Sammy all work for legacy media outlets (Reuters and the NYT). I notice there arent many movies with independent writers as protagonists. Back in the late 2000s and early 2010s, movies and shows would sometimes portray bloggers as main characters. Because the political climate has changed, legacy media is now seen in Hollywood as higher-status than being a Substacker or a YouTuber. This movie would have been more interesting if, say, Jessie had been a livestreamer with a GoPro who conflicted with her mentor Lee, an old-school photojournalist.
Nick Offerman is depicted as the U.S. president who has disbanded the FBI and is now serving a third term. Clearly meant to be a stand-in for Trump.
Nick Offerman in Civil War (2024).
.
This fictional president speaks in Trumpian rhetoric but he has the glib demeanor and over-gelled hairstyle of California governor Gavin Newsom.
At the end of the movie, the president is killed by the Western Forces and Joel, a character we the audience have grown to like, is clearly pleased by this. I view this as a kind of fantasy for a small number of progressive viewers who, of course, would abhor a civil war but would also, if such a scenario were to arise, enjoy the thought of the former president whimpering as sympathetically-portrayed rebel soldiers standing up to a fascist regime emptied their magazines into his body.
The JCPenny parking lot scene early in the movie. The lot is abandoned and in ruins. The first thought that came to mind was This still looks better than a lot of actual JCPenny parking lots today.
Early in the movie, the Antifa massacre is mentioned. Several reviewers and commentators have noticed that cleverly, the movie never makes clear whether this means that Antifa committed a massacre or were the victims of a massacre. My question: Given that Antifa is largely concentrated in metropolitan cities in which they are tightly and intricately networked, whats more likelythat during a period of the breakdown of law and order, they committed a massacre or were the victims of one?
Theres a scene where the main characters arrive in a peaceful town in Pennsylvania (Pittsburgh, I think). They are all amazed at how calm and civil this town is. Then Sammy, the old journalist, points out that there are armed men on the rooftops, and makes a remark about how you wouldnt want to live in such a place. Well. Lets be serious. All peace is maintained with the implicit threat of violence. You cant ever eliminate violence or its threat. The best any society can do is determine how much violence and what kind. Right now, at this very moment, people armed with guns are the reason laws can be enforced, to the extent that they are. Just because you dont literally see them on rooftops doesnt mean they dont exist.
I appreciated that the sounds of gunfire in Civil War were realistic. So many guns in Hollywood movies have that explosive, bass-boosted sound. In real life shootings, the press often reports that witnesses were taken by surprise upon hearing gunfire, at first associating the noise with firecrackers or a popping sound. Hollywood movies have convinced the public that gunshots have a high-pitched, loud boom followed by an echo.
This leads me to believe that this movie was largely meant to be a warning to the movie-watching public: We should try to avoid a civil war and find a way to resolve our differences before we reach the point where public order breaks down, the country fragments into Western Forces, Portland Maoists, Loyalist States, and so on.
Its a decent popcorn movie. At this point, I welcome any popcorn movie that isnt based on a comic book or video game. Its somewhat original. Thats enough for me.
