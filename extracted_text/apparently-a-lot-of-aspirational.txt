I needed a job when I was in high school. I filled out applications for Taco Bell, Grocery Outlet, and a few other places. Eventually, I landed a job at a local Italian restaurant as a busboy and dishwasher. This job sucked about as much as you would expect. Still, it was better than all the manual-labor jobs Id done before because it was indoors. From ages 11 to 15, I would rake leaves. Clean gutters. Stack firewood. Dig ditches to install sprinklers. Those jobs were toughworking out in the scorching California sun and drinking water out of a rubber hose. By the time I got a normal job washing dishes (normal meaning youre paying taxes), I was grateful to be indoors in a place that had air conditioning and predictable hours. And not have to ride in the back of a pickup truck to the local landfill.
And I liked my restaurant coworkers. Most were in their early twenties, some in their thirties. My manager, Jim, was a small guy with a mustache who was in his forties. During my interview with him, he explained that he had been in prison but that the owner of the restaurant thought he deserved a second chance and hired him to manage the restaurant. Jim was very loyal to the owner because of this. He worked harder than anyone Id ever met to ensure that the restaurant operated smoothly.
Yes the job sucked but the psychological effect of being employed, of earning my own money, of having slightly older coworkers, flirting with older waitresses, busing tables and interacting with patrons in an adult-adult manner rather than being treated as a kid, gave me a remarkable shot of confidence as a 15 year old kid. I received several raises in the first few months on the job, along with much praise from the restaurant owner, which confused me at the time. But in hindsight, I can understand why. Just showing up and doing the job consistently and without complaint will put you ahead of just about everyone else who works those kind of jobs.
On breaks, all my conversations with coworkers eventually turned to the topic of money, or lack of it. We would fantasize about what we would do if we suddenly struck it rich (a few coworkers played the lottery): vacations, cars, a house by the beach. I was making minimum wage, which was then $6.75 an hour. The manager Jim and the owner both noticed how efficiently I worked.
I was this awkward 15-year-old kid. Jim pulls me to the side. He had this large mustache and he had this large tattoo on his forearm. Hed done some time in prison. And he got out. He started working at this restaurant. The owner liked him because of how hard he worked. Made him an assistant manager and then the manager. Jim had been working there for something 10 years by the time I started working there. He pulls me to the side and he says, Rob, youre doing a great job here. Were going to give you a raise. A couple weeks after that, he does the same thing. Gives me another raise. Eventually, Im making more than a lot of the pizza makers. I just thought, okay, well, Im washing dishes. Im doing my job. I felt a little awkward that he was praising me so much for doing such a simple job so well. But then I would hear stories about other dishwashers who worked there when I wasnt on the clock, and how they would take frequent smoke breaks, how they would go to the bathroom a lot, and how essentially they would do everything they could to avoid doing the actual job they were being paid to do. And even at that young age, I slowly came to realize that it doesnt take that much work to stand out. If you just do your job without complaint and do it well, people will take notice.
I liked that I was making my own money, and I especially liked getting tips.When Id bus the tables, Id collect the tips left by patrons and stuff them into my apron. Then, at the end of the shift, Id pull out these dollar bills from my apron, soaking wet from an evening of washing dishes, and split them with my coworkers.
Eventually, I wanted to quit my job at the restaurant. I had grown weary of washing dishes, to the point where the smell of tomato sauce made my stomach turn. I associated it with dirty dishes and half eaten plates of pasta. The only time something exciting happened was when I was washing dishes, heard some glasses shatter, and looked out into the dining area. Two guys I knew from my high school were in a fist fight, and my manager broke it up and told them to leave or hed call the cops.
Later I got a job at a supermarket bagging groceries. I was so relievedstocking shelves and bagging groceries were not the most glamourous tasks, but they beat scrubbing dishes and trying to get the smell of onions and garlic out of my fingernails. I had to put in my two weeks notice at the restaurant. And I remember being nervous when I had to tell my manager, Jim, about this. And he was happy for me. He said, no, this is good. I know no one wants to be working at a restaurant washing dishes. You want every job you get to be better than the job you had before. And thats what youre doing. You went from washing dishes, now youre going to bag groceries, and hopefully the next job after that will be something even better. And that was the advice that I needed at that time. And I could easily imagine some journalist or some sociology professor or anon poster on X witnessing that interaction and scoffing and saying, oh, you have this kid in this run down, working class town, making minimum wage. He went from one dead end job to another. Hes trapped in these impersonal and powerful forces he cant understand or control. Hes a wage slave. A lot of right wing online people would call me a wage cuck. Someone whos living paycheck to paycheck, trying to make ends meet. If Id heard something like that as a teenager, it would have just demoralized me. But thanks to Jim, I felt I was advancing in the world. I wanted that feeling again.

Reading those comments, Im so glad that online culture didnt exist when I was a teenager. I could easily imagine that changing my attitude toward hard work, especially as someone who didnt have much in the way of mentorship or guidance or adult role models or a father around. Social media hardly existed at that point in 2006. It was MySpace and not much else.
But today, if youre a 15 or 16 year old kid, and youre seeing comments to Christopher Rufos post that yeah, you can work at Panda Express and make 70k if you stay with the job long enough and become assistant manager. And the comments are, oh, this is an absurd thing to post. The job market is so bad. Whats the point? This isnt it. All the Twitter or social media lingo. Theres one commenter that wrote learn to burrito is the new learn to code in reference to the Chipotle job opening. Its mockery. Its demoralizing.
This attitude and hostility toward work. You see it a lot on the political left. Thats part of the package of ideas about how work is exploitation and how and we need to overthrow the system to create a more equitable and just society. But Im mystified to see that this attitude also exists on the right as well. Theres always this danger of overinterpreting comments on social media, but it does bleed over into the real world. When you talk to young people, they do spend a lot of time online and they do absorb this stuff.
This whole line of thinking makes my blood boil because a lot of these people think theyre too good for this work. Tracing Woodgrains, he called them temporarily embarrassed aristocrats, which I think goes a long way to explaining the reaction to Rufos post.
Regardless of political orientation, many aspirational elites have more respect for criminals than for ordinary people working ordinary jobs. They consider menial work humiliating and easily imagine, in response to such indignities, committing crimes.
They could easily imagine committing crimes rather than face the humiliation of going to a job where you have to clock in and wear a uniform and put on a visor and an apron and get to work.
More and more young people think theyre above this kind of work. But heres the thing: even if you want to be successful in a white-collar job, or be an online influencer, or whatever it is you think is good enough for you, theres no way you can truly appreciate that position without spending some time in tough circumstances first. It doesnt have to be working in fast food or washing dishes like I did. But maybe a few months as a camp counselor or mopping floors or in some other role where you have to show up to a place you dont want to be, follow orders, and learn how an organization works. You see how a hierarchy functions, where older people have more experience and you need to learn from them. The main reason I can really value what I do now is because of those earlier jobs. If youre handed everything right away, you wont appreciate itand in many cases, youll just squander it. Sadly thats a kind of lesson you cant be told; you have to experience it.

Relatedly, I also dislike when writers (or influencers or musicians or other creatives) say things like, This is the only thing Ive ever been good at. It perpetuates the view that creativity or writing is a gift rather than something that must be worked at constantly. Yes, you need some bare minimum level of ability to be a good writer, but not as much as you might think. It also implies that the writer can only do things he or she is good at. Most people arent working jobs which demand some unique talent. Yes, you are fortunate to make a living writing. But if you couldnt, its not as if youd be forever jobless. Youd figure it out and wait tables or paint parking lots or file documents like everyone else. Youre not that special.
Even in high school, I remember the constant drumbeat about college: "College graduates earn a million dollars more over their lifetime than non-college graduates, so you better go to college." A lot of young people heard that and thought, Oh, if I go to college, Ill be a millionaire. It sounds stupid in hindsight, but when youre 17 and dont know much, thats not an unreasonable conclusion to draw. The reality, though, is that hard work is always going to be part of the equation. Sure, you can earn a million dollars as a college graduate, but its going to take decades of work and effortits not like you get your bachelors degree and someone hands you a trust fund.
This helps explain whats going on with the dissident right and these young people who think, If I have XYZ attributes, society will just hand me credentials, money, or status. Like, Oh, I have this IQ score, Im this percent conscientious, and I went to this kind of schoolso obviously, Im owed something. But no, you still have to work. You can have two people with the same IQ and level of conscientiousness, but the person who applies those traits to something meaningful is going to be more successful than the one who just stares at their "scorecard" and wonders why life isnt working out. Having good stats on paper means nothing if you dont actually put in the effort.
Theres an idea from Jordan Peterson about how too often we get our questions backwards. The question isnt Why arent young people working hard? Its Why did young people ever work hard at all to begin with? Where did that work ethic come from in generations past or decades past? A lot of that was because we had a culture that conferred status to hard work, that had adults and role models who said hard work was important. As that attitude has evolved, young people are responding accordingly. Its not as if the default is that humans are all hard workers. Were going to try to do as little as we possibly can unless theres reason not to. Were seeing that now.
A lot of people expect their job to give their life meaning. But for most people, youre not going to find deep fulfillment from being the assistant manager at Chipotleand thats okay. In the past, people found meaning in other areas. Arthur Brooks talks about the four pillars of meaning: faith, family, vocation, and community. If youre ambitious, talented, hardworking, and creative, you can find meaning in your job. You can land a creative, interesting, and lucrative role that gives you fulfillment. But for most peopleeven those who are above average in many waystheir primary source of meaning isnt going to come from their job. Historically, it came from one of those other three pillars: having a family, contributing to their community, or being involved in a religious or social organization where they felt useful and connected. But as those other sources of meaning have been mocked and ridiculed and harder to access, work has become the last pillar standing.
So now, work has to be everythingits expected to be the main source of purpose in life. And for most people, thats just not realistic. I think weve made a mistake by overemphasizing that one pillar while neglecting the others. So much of the guidance people receive is focused on getting a good education, landing a great job, and building a careerassuming that the rewards and purpose will come later. But most people wont have thrilling or glamorous careers. Most people will have ordinary jobs where they clock in and clock out. And if they dont have a strong community, a family, or something outside of work that gives their life meaning, theyre going to feel empty and hopeless.
I dont imagine were all going to come to our senses and voluntarily grow up. Either war or economic catastrophe or something elsemaybe a pandemic that actually affects young peoplethat requires them to sacrifice something. That was one of the crazy things about COVID-19: young people, especially from middle class families and above, werent asked to sacrifice anything. They were just asked to stay at home, and a lot of them preferred that. In some ways, their lives even improved. For university students, it was basically impossible to fail your classes at that point. Professors and graduate students were pressured to pass every student. They came out of this pandemic softer. I dont know if thats ever been the case in the history of the world, where there was a global pandemic and young people actually came out of it weaker rather than stronger. Maybe some other event will change things.
We have to respect the dignity of work itself. Working any job is better than wasting away on your couch. Giving everyone a universal basic income (UBI) isnt going to magically turn people into Mozarts or Hemingways. Most jobless people arent spending their time painting or sculptingtheyre watching TV or playing video games. Some people shy away from making value judgments about that, but lets be honest: its not a great way to spend your time. A lot of UBI proponents assume that if youre naturally interesting and creative, a guaranteed income will give you the freedom to focus on your passions without worrying about paying the bills. And maybe thats true for some people.
But for most people, work itself is where theyll find meaningor at least where theyll get the structure and momentum to discover it.
This boils down to two competing ideas about human nature.
Oversimplifying: the left generally believes that once peoples basic needs are metonce theyre fed, warm, sheltered, and comfortabletheyll be free to pursue meaning and fulfillment. Theyll finally be able to thrive.
The old-school conservative view, in contrast, is that the process of working to meet your material needsearning your way, being self-sufficient, and taking care of yourself and othersis what actually creates meaning and fulfillment. Even if its uncomfortable in the moment, the act of doing the work unlocks a deeper sense of purpose. That view seems more accurate to me. If you look at anxiety and depression rates, theyre often higher in first-world countries where people have material abundance. Meanwhile, people in many developing countriesdespite facing real hardshipsoften report a stronger sense of purpose and fulfillment. That tells you something about the role that effort, struggle, and self-sufficiency play in living a meaningful life.
Most people whove been through trial-by-fire experiences are grateful for the growth, even if they hated it at the time. There are very few things in life that make you grow while youre feeling a 10 out of 10 on the pleasure scale. I remember those jobswashing dishes, bagging groceries, collecting carts, basic training and my job in the Air ForceI didn't like any of it while experiencing it. I saw a TikTok once of a young Army recruit filming his fellow new recruits. He asked, "If you could tell your recruiter anything, what would you say?" And these kids were saying theyd tell him to fuck off because they felt misled. I get it. In that moment, it feels awful. But five years from nowespecially ten years from nowtheyll be glad they went through it. Its the same thing with going to the gym. In the moment, lifting weights doesnt feel great. The process leading up to it and the act itself can suck. But after youre doneand especially after youve kept up with ityoure happy you did it. Were losing that sense of delayed gratification in an era of instant rewards. You have to do a lot of pushups before you see any progress, but post a silly video online, and youll get a few hundred likes in an instant. That difference shapes how we approach effortand whether we stick with things that actually make us grow.
